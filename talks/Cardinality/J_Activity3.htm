    Discussing the growth of a sequence

    Jenny: When could we say that a sequence “grows faster” than another one?

    Ivan: When the difference between two consecutive terms of the first one is bigger than the difference of the corresponding terms of the second.

    Jenny: Take the sequences an = 2n and bn=1000*n.  Let us consider the first several differences: a2 – a1 = 2 and  b2 – b1 = 1000, a3 – a2 = 4 and  b3 – b2 = 1000 but for n = 11:

    a11 – a10 = 2048-1024 = 1024 and  b11 – b10 = 1000. So? Would you like to reformulate your definition?

    Ivan (thinks hard).

    Jenny: This is not an easy question. You could think it over and let me know later.

    Ivan: O.K. I’d rather think a little bit more then…

    George: Imagine a car of the future which moves with a velocity of 1000 km/h. Let it start at noon. What will the sequence of the distances it covers at each exact hour be?

    Ivan: 1000, 2000, 3000, …

    George: Can you model its movement in Excel?

    Ivan: Sure! Here it is:

    Ivan: The sequence of the differences of the first sequence is constant – 1000, and the sequence of the differences of the second sequence changes – it grows.

    George: What about the sequence of the differences of the differences?

    Ivan: It is 0 in the first case but not in the second one.

    Jenny: Can you play with the sequence of the differences so as to change the behaviour of the original sequence?

    Ivan: I’ll augment each element of the sequence of the differences of the differences by 1, 2, 3, etc. and see what becomes of the original sequence {1000*n}. 

    George: We could denote this new sequence by (1’). It is interesting to compare (1’) with {2n}.

    Ivan: The sequence of the differences of the differences of {2n} coincides with the original sequence.

    George:  Thus the sequence of the differences of the differences in both cases will grow but if we make one more step we’ll get a sequence of differences which is constant for the new sequence (1’).

    Jenny: So, what do you think now about the comparison of any two sequences? If two sequences are growing when could we say that the first one “grows faster” than the second one?

    Ivan: We should look at the sequence of the differences of the differences. If in both cases these differences are growing we could say nothing. We should look at the differences of the differences of the differences so many times until one of them stops growing or becomes decreasing.

    Jenny: And how could we relate this to the movement of two cars? If both of them have constant velocity what is their acceleration?

    Ivan: 0.

    Jenny: If both of them are accelerating?

    Ivan: We should study the sequence of the differences of the their accelerations…

  • Note: See how refined the definition of "growth" of a sequence becomes in the case of Ivan. In fact he is talking about finite differences of a higher order in how own words. Relating this to the real world phenomena is also very important.

    1.When can you say two infinite sequences are the same size?

    Teddy: Never, because they are infinite and we don’t know the number of their terms.

    Ivan, Mitty: Depends on what you mean by “size”.

    Teddy: What do WE mean by “size” then?

    George: What would you suggest as a measure for the size of a sequence?

    Ivan: This could be the difference of the consecutive terms of the sequence.

    George: Anything else?

    Mitty: I think that we could define three things for determining when one sequence has a greater size than another:

    v     The number of the terms;

    v     The value of the terms;

    v     The distance between consecutive terms

    Ivan: I agree, if we could say that there are more terms in a sequence than in another one, the first sequence would be of a greater size.

    Mitty: On the other hand, if the distance between the consecutive terms of a sequence is small, i.e. if it grows slowly, and the [corresponding] distance between the consecutive terms of another sequence is bigger we could say that the second one is bigger.

    Teddy: This would mean that there are infinities of different size. I think that the number of the terms in a sequence could be bigger than the number of the terms in another one though…

    Note:  Here the problem might be with the term we use in Bulgarian (but also even in English). The term “преброявам” we have used corresponds to: “count up”, “take count of”. In Bulgarian this conveys the flavor of a process which should be finished and this contradicts to the notion of “infinite”. A synonym of this verb is “изброявам” which is “to enumerate, to list”. I think that if we had said instead “enumerate” the answers would be different…
